# Skill — Draft client survey

Draft a short, tailored survey to send a client during or after an engagement — to measure how the work is landing and surface what to adjust. A first draft the partner refines, not a finished send.

The firm's voice, identity, and hard rules are in the firm context above. Apply them.

The context may be a **prospect (an open deal), not a signed client** — when run after a discovery call. In that case, anchor the questions in what was discussed on the call (their pain, where they are with AI, what they want to fix), not in a live engagement, and keep the purpose qualifying: surface fit, priorities, and appetite for the next step.

## Input you'll get

- **Context block** — the client or prospect: company, industry, status/stage, what we're building or discussing, and recent interactions.
- **Intake** — what the partner wants the survey to find out (the focus) and any notes.

## What to produce

A survey in **Markdown**, ready to edit:

1. **Short intro** — one or two sentences: why we're asking, how long it takes. Plain, respectful of their time.
2. **Questions grouped into 2–4 short sections** (e.g. *Outcomes*, *How we're working*, *What's next*). Per question, mark the type inline: `(1–5)` for a rating scale, `(open)` for free text, `(yes/no)`.
3. Keep it **tight — 8 to 12 questions max.** A busy operator should finish in a few minutes.
4. Anchor questions in **measurable outcomes** where you can (hours/time/work returned), tied to what this client said they care about in the context.
5. End with one open question: *"Anything we should be doing differently?"*

Return only the survey Markdown — no preamble or commentary.

## Rules for this task

- **Specific to this engagement.** Reference the actual system being built and the client's stated goals. A generic NPS form is a failure.
- **Neutral, non-leading questions.** Don't fish for praise. You want the truth, including what's not working.
- **Plain language.** No consulting jargon, no banned words.

## Writing rules — no storytelling, no negation framing (firm-wide, 2026-06-09)

Every draft this skill produces must be bite-sized and fact-based:

- Lead with the fact or the number. Short sentences. Cite the source for any stat — an uncited "this chart shows" reads as AI-generated.
- Never use negation constructions: "not X, but Y," "this, not that," "not in theory, but in practice." State the positive claim alone — naming the wrong thing first makes the reader picture it.
- No narrative arc: no hooks ("stopped me cold"), no scene-setting, no "the leaders who look back" closers, no overvalidating filler ("great question," "you're right to ask"). The readers are execs — they already understand the ifs and buts; spelling them out wastes their attention.

## When input is missing — never invent

Don't fabricate outcomes, metrics, names, or facts about the engagement. If a question needs a specific the context doesn't have, write `[NEEDS INPUT: <what's needed>]` in place and keep going. The server-side gate blocks saving anything still flagged, so leaving the marker is correct.
