# Skill — Discovery call prep (internal)

Write an **internal** prep doc for a Shift partner about to run a discovery call with a prospect. This is for our own team — not the client. The goal of the call is to get to know them, gauge what they know about AI, qualify them, and earn the next call (a discussion call). Your job: a tight, confidence-giving brief the partner can skim before they dial in.

The firm's voice, identity, and hard rules are in the firm context above. Apply them.

## Input you'll get

- **Context block** — the opportunity: company, industry, deal stage/value, the primary contact (name, title), lead source, and recent interactions.
- **Intake** — what the partner wants out of this call and anything specific to weave in.

## What to produce

Markdown, internal-facing, scannable in under a minute. These sections, in this order, nothing else:

1. **Quick read** — 3–5 bullets: who this person is and the company, the deal value/stage, where the lead came from, last touch. Just the load-bearing facts from context.
2. **Agenda (flow, not clock)** — the *shape* of the call as an ordered flow, one line each: open + build rapport → **let them describe their world first** (operations, team, what's painful) → dig into the pain (cost, time, where it hurts) → where systems + AI could fit → how they decide / who else is involved → close on a concrete next step. No timings.
3. **Questions that qualify** — 4–6 sharp questions, in the firm's plain voice, that surface budget, authority, real pain, and timeline ("what's this costing you a week?", "who else weighs in on something like this?", "what have you already tried?"). Tie them to what we know about this prospect.
4. **How to run it** — 3–5 tactical tips drawn from the field notes below, made specific to this prospect: gauge their AI maturity before choosing pitch depth, which single demo fits their named pain, when to raise security/ownership, listen more than pitch, get one concrete number. Don't scope or price on the call.
5. **Win the next call** — what a good outcome looks like and the close, three parts: **name the playbook out loud** (discovery → discussion doc with no pricing → proposal), **give the homework** (ask them to list pain points as plain user stories — "as a dispatcher I don't want…" — not solutions; suggest a short internal team chat to build the list), and **book the next meeting before hanging up** — a date on the calendar, not "in a couple weeks."
6. **Watch-outs** — 2–3 one-line reminders picked from the field notes for THIS prospect (e.g. "early on AI — keep the altitude low, one step at a time" or "already price-anchored — don't reopen the number").

Return only the brief — no preamble.

## Field notes — what real discovery calls taught us (reviewed 2026-06-09)

Distilled from transcripts of three real Shift calls. They advanced — but none were perfect, and the partner's own debrief flagged several of these. Use them to write sections 4–6; pick what fits this prospect, don't dump the whole list.

**Repeat — these worked every time they were used:**
- **Let them talk first.** The best stretches came from open prompts ("what gets you excited about this?"). A prospect describing their dream in their own words is selling themselves; interrupting that to pitch loses ground.
- **Gauge AI maturity, then match altitude.** An operator early on AI gets overwhelmed by the full company-transformation vision — it visibly cost ground on one call and had to be walked back. Probe what they use today before choosing how big to talk.
- **One demo, theirs.** One tool, chosen for the pain they named, walked slowly. A fast tour of everything reads as fuzz ("it became fuzzy very quickly" is how one prospect described AI pitches generally — don't be that).
- **Raise security, data residency, and IP ownership proactively.** It landed on every call it appeared — risk-averse operators relax when told the data stays put, the client owns the code, and AI never auto-sends anything. Don't wait to be asked.
- **Name the playbook.** Saying the road out loud — discovery → discussion doc (no pricing) → proposal — relaxed every prospect who heard it. People say yes to a next step they can see.
- **Phase-one framing de-risks.** "One tool, three months, real ROI, then we decide together what's next" repeatedly converted big-vision anxiety into agreement.
- **Funding flips the cost conversation.** For Canadian prospects with budget sensitivity, SHRED/IRAP turned "can we afford this" into "the government pays part" — flag it in the brief when it plausibly applies.

**Avoid — these hurt and recurred:**
- **Pitching on the first buying signal.** One hint of interest is not permission for the full vision. Stay in questions longer than feels natural.
- **Monologues.** Multi-minute uninterrupted runs showed up on every call. The brief should remind the partner: their voice under half the airtime.
- **Walking in cold.** One call was run admittedly unprepped. This brief is the fix — it only works if it's read before the dial.
- **Naming other clients or their numbers.** Real client names, their vendor quotes, their acquisition plans, and third parties' economics were disclosed on these calls. Use sanitized examples ("a 270-person engineering firm") unless the client has cleared it.
- **Oversharing firm internals.** Partner disputes, rates, equity splits, "we changed our email three times this week" — never on a recorded prospect call. We are a senior firm; sound like one.
- **Hanging up without a booked date.** All three calls ended on "next couple weeks." Vague timing is where deals stall — the close in section 5 exists because of this.

## Rules for this task

- **Internal only.** This is never sent to the client. Be direct and tactical.
- **Specific to this prospect.** Generic call advice is a failure — ground every section in the context.
- **Plain language, no jargon, no banned words.**
- **Short.** A partner reads this in the elevator. Bullets over prose.

## When input is missing — never invent

Don't fabricate facts about the person or company. If a section needs a specific the context doesn't have, write `[NEEDS INPUT: <what's needed>]` in place and keep going. The server-side gate blocks saving anything still flagged, so leaving the marker is correct.
